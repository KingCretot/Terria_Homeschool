📖 Key Terms & Definitions

Click any flip card to reveal the definition!

Inequality Model
An inequality used to represent a real-world situation with a range of solutions (e.g., cost ≤ budget)
At Least
Means ≥ (greater than or equal to). Example: "You need at least 80 points" → score ≥ 80
At Most
Means ≤ (less than or equal to). Example: "Spend at most $50" → cost ≤ 50
No More Than
Same as "at most" → ≤. Example: "no more than 5 items" → items ≤ 5
More Than / Exceeds
Means > (strictly greater than). Example: "income exceeds $500" → income > 500
Solution Set
All values that make an inequality true. Graphed on a number line with an arrow showing the range.
Pollutant
A harmful substance introduced into the environment. In 3.AP, we calculate concentrations and allowable levels.
Concentration
The amount of a substance per unit of space (e.g., mg per liter). Used to measure pollution levels.
Stewardship
Responsible management of something entrusted to one's care — including the environment. (Biblical concept: Genesis 2:15)

3.7 — USING INEQUALITIES
📐 Applying Inequalities to Real-World Problems

Just like we used equations to model real situations in 3.5, we now use inequalities to model situations where there's a range of acceptable values — like a budget, a minimum score, or a speed limit.

🔑 The Four-Step Model Works for Inequalities Too!
Step 1: Read carefully. Step 2: Define the variable. Step 3: Write and solve the inequality. Step 4: Check + interpret.
📘 Example 1 — Budget Problem
Real-World Situation:

You have $85 to spend on new school supplies. A backpack costs $35. How many notebooks at $5 each can you also buy?

1
Define: Let n = number of notebooks
2
Write: 35 + 5n ≤ 85 (total cost must be AT MOST $85)
3
Solve: Subtract 35 → 5n ≤ 50 → Divide by 5 → n ≤ 10
4
Interpret: You can buy 10 or fewer notebooks. (n can be 0, 1, 2, … 10)
Graph: n ≤ 10
📘 Example 2 — Minimum Score
Real-World Situation:

Terria needs an average of at least 85 on two tests to get an A. She scored 78 on the first test. What must she score on the second test?

1
Define: Let s = score on second test
2
Write: (78 + s) ÷ 2 ≥ 85 (average must be AT LEAST 85)
3
Multiply both sides by 2: 78 + s ≥ 170
4
Subtract 78: s ≥ 92
5
Interpret: Terria must score 92 or higher on the second test!
📘 Example 3 — Speed Limit / Distance
Real-World Situation:

A truck driver must travel more than 300 miles before stopping. If he drives at 60 mph, how many hours must he drive? Write an inequality.

1
Define: Let h = hours driven; distance = 60h
2
Write: 60h > 300 (distance must be MORE THAN 300)
3
Divide by 60 (positive, no flip): h > 5
4
Interpret: He must drive MORE than 5 hours (open circle at 5, arrow right)
💡 Remember These Key Words:
"at least / minimum / no less than" → ≥   |   "at most / maximum / no more than" → ≤   |   "exceeds / more than" → >   |   "below / under / fewer than" → <
APPLICATION PROBLEMS — MANAGING POLLUTANTS
🌿 3.AP — Environmental Stewardship & Math
BIBLICAL WORLDVIEW CONNECTION

"The LORD God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it." — Genesis 2:15

Math helps us be responsible stewards of the environment. By calculating pollution levels, scientists and engineers can determine how much of a harmful substance is safe — and when action is needed.

📊 3.AP.1 — Calculating Pollution Concentration Values
Background:

Industrial facilities release pollutants into rivers and air. Scientists measure concentration — the amount of pollutant per volume (like mg per liter, or mg/L). The EPA sets maximum allowable concentrations to protect ecosystems and human health.

Example Problem 1 — Water Pollutant:
1
Situation: A factory can release at most 0.05 mg/L of a chemical into a river. The current concentration is 0.02 mg/L. How much more (x) can be safely added?
2
Write inequality: 0.02 + x ≤ 0.05
3
Subtract 0.02: x ≤ 0.03
4
Interpret: At most 0.03 mg/L more can be safely added to the river.
Example Problem 2 — Pollution Reduction:
1
Situation: A city produces 80 tons of pollution per year. New regulations require reducing pollution to fewer than 50 tons. Each new filter reduces pollution by 6 tons. How many filters (f) are needed?
2
Write inequality: 80 − 6f < 50
3
Subtract 80: −6f < −30
4
Divide by −6 (NEGATIVE → FLIP!): f > 5
5
Interpret: The city needs MORE than 5 filters — so at least 6 filters to meet regulations.
🌱 3.AP.2 — Biblical Principle: Exercising Dominion

The term "exercising dominion" (from Genesis 1:28) means wisely managing the earth — not exploiting it. This includes:

  • Setting maximum pollution limits to protect ecosystems
  • Using math to find the minimum number of filters needed for clean water
  • Calculating whether pollutant levels are within safe (inequality) ranges
  • Making responsible decisions for future generations

Math isn't just numbers — it's a tool for fulfilling our responsibility to care for creation.

Practice — Try It Yourself:
Challenge:

An air quality standard requires NO MORE THAN 12 parts per million (ppm) of a pollutant. A factory currently produces 20 ppm. Each new scrubber installed reduces pollution by 2.5 ppm. How many scrubbers (s) must be installed?

(Try it on your whiteboard. Check: 20 − 2.5s ≤ 12 → answer: s ≥ 3.2 → at least 4 scrubbers)
