Section 3.1

Solving by Adding & Subtracting

Whatever you do to one side, do to the other.

Goal
An equation is a balance scale ⚖️. The two sides are equal. To solve it, get the variable (like x) all alone on one side. We call that isolating the variable.
The move
If a number is being added to the variable, subtract it from both sides. If a number is being subtracted, add it to both sides. These opposites are called inverse operations.

✨ Worked Examples

x + 7 = 12
  1. The +7 is stuck to x → do the opposite
  2. Subtract 7 from BOTH sides
  3. x + 7 − 7 = 12 − 7
  4. x = 5
n − 4 = 9
  1. The −4 is attached to n → add 4
  2. Add 4 to BOTH sides
  3. n − 4 + 4 = 9 + 4
  4. n = 13
15 = y + 6
  1. Variable can live on the right too
  2. Subtract 6 from both sides
  3. 15 − 6 = y
  4. y = 9
💡 Check it! Put your answer back in: 5 + 7 = 12 ✅. If both sides match, you nailed it.

Section 3.2

Solving by Multiplying & Dividing

A number stuck to the variable by × or ÷? Undo it.

Coefficient
In 6n, the 6 is the coefficient — it's multiplying n. To undo multiplication, divide both sides by that number.
Division
If the variable is being divided (like x⁄4), multiply both sides to undo it. Always do the SAME thing to both sides.

✨ Worked Examples

4x = 36
  1. 4 is multiplying x → divide by 4
  2. 4x ÷ 4 = 36 ÷ 4
  3. x = 9
x⁄3 = 6
  1. x is divided by 3 → multiply by 3
  2. (x⁄3) × 3 = 6 × 3
  3. x = 18
5y = 45
  1. Divide both sides by 5
  2. 5y ÷ 5 = 45 ÷ 5
  3. y = 9
💡 Check: 4 × 9 = 36 ✅. Multiplying back should rebuild the original.

Section 3.3

Two-Step Equations

Undo +/− first, then undo ×/÷. Reverse of order of operations.

Two layers
A two-step equation like 2x + 5 = 13 has the variable wrapped in TWO operations. Unwrap it like a present — outer layer first.
The order
Step 1: undo the adding or subtracting. Step 2: undo the multiplying or dividing. This is the reverse of PEMDAS.

✨ Worked Examples

2x + 5 = 13
  1. Step 1: subtract 5 → 2x = 8
  2. Step 2: divide by 2 → x = 4
3x − 4 = 17
  1. Step 1: add 4 → 3x = 21
  2. Step 2: divide by 3 → x = 7
x⁄2 + 3 = 7
  1. Step 1: subtract 3 → x⁄2 = 4
  2. Step 2: multiply by 2 → x = 8
💡 Check: 2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13 ✅.

Section 3.4

Simplifying Before Solving

Tidy up each side FIRST, then solve.

Like terms
Like terms have the same variable: 3x and 2x are like terms. Combine them by adding the coefficients: 3x + 2x = 5x.
Distribute
The distributive property means a number outside parentheses multiplies everything inside: 2(x + 3) = 2x + 6. Spread it to BOTH terms.

✨ Worked Examples

3x + 2x + 4 = 24
  1. Combine like terms: 5x + 4 = 24
  2. Subtract 4 → 5x = 20
  3. Divide by 5 → x = 4
2(x + 3) = 16
  1. Distribute: 2x + 6 = 16
  2. Subtract 6 → 2x = 10
  3. Divide by 2 → x = 5
4x − x + 5 = 20
  1. Combine: 3x + 5 = 20
  2. Subtract 5 → 3x = 15
  3. Divide by 3 → x = 5
💡 Always simplify each side BEFORE you start moving things across the equals sign.

Section 3.5

Using Equations (Word Problems)

Turn the words into an equation, then solve it.

Translate
Key words become math: “more than / plus” → +, “less than” → −, “times / of” → ×, “divided / per” → ÷, “is / equals” → =.
Plan
Step 1: let a letter stand for the unknown. Step 2: write the equation. Step 3: solve. Step 4: answer in a sentence.

✨ Worked Examples

“A number times 3, plus 5, equals 20.”
  1. Let the number = n
  2. 3n + 5 = 20
  3. Subtract 5 → 3n = 15
  4. Divide by 3 → n = 5
“7 less than a number is 12.”
  1. Let the number = n
  2. n − 7 = 12
  3. Add 7 → n = 19
“Terria saved $8 each week and now has $56. How many weeks?”
  1. Let weeks = w
  2. 8w = 56
  3. Divide by 8 → w = 7 weeks
💡 “Less than” flips the order: “7 less than n” is n − 7, NOT 7 − n. Watch for it!

Section 3.6

Solving Inequalities

Solve just like an equation — but FLIP the sign for a negative × or ÷.

Symbols
> greater than, < less than, ≥ greater-than-or-equal (“at least”), ≤ less-than-or-equal (“at most”). Unlike an equation, the answer is a whole range of numbers — so we picture it on a number line.
The flip rule
Solve the same way you solve equations: undo +/−, then ×/÷. BUT if you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign (< becomes >, ≤ becomes ≥).
Why a range?
“x > 6” isn’t one answer — it’s 6.1, 7, 100, and every number bigger than 6. That’s why we shade a direction on the line instead of marking a single point.

📏 Graphing the Solution on a Number Line

An inequality has many answers, so we picture the whole range on a number line. Follow these four steps every time:

  1. Solve until the variable is alone on the left (for example, x > 6).
  2. Find the boundary number on the line and put your dot there.
  3. Choose the dot. Use an open dot ○ for < or > (the boundary is not a solution). Use a closed dot ● for ≤ or ≥ (the boundary is a solution).
  4. Shade with an arrow toward every number that works — to the right for > or ≥, to the left for < or ≤.

💡 Quick dot check: does the symbol have a little line under it (≤ or ≥)? Line under → closed dot ●. Plain < or > → open dot ○.

✨ Worked Examples

x + 4 > 10
  1. Subtract 4 from both sides
  2. x > 6
  3. Open dot ○ at 6 (6 is NOT a solution), shade right
Graph:
3x ≤ 18
  1. Divide by 3 (positive → no flip)
  2. x ≤ 6
  3. Closed dot ● at 6 (6 IS a solution), shade left
Graph:
−2x < 8
  1. Divide by −2 → FLIP the sign!
  2. x > −4
  3. Open dot ○ at −4, shade right
Graph:

Section 3.7

Using Inequalities (Real World)

“At most” → ≤, “at least” → ≥. Build it, then solve it.

The phrases
“at most / no more than / maximum” → ≤. “at least / no less than / minimum” → ≥. “under / below / fewer than” → <. “over / above / more than” → >.
Set it up
Write what’s happening, then solve like a two-step inequality. Your answer is a range — so finish by graphing it on a number line.
Dots in real life
“At most / at least” include the boundary, so they use a closed dot ●. “Below / above” do not, so they use an open dot ○. In word problems the answer usually also can’t go below 0 — you can’t have −2 buses!

📏 Graphing the Solution on a Number Line

An inequality has many answers, so we picture the whole range on a number line. Follow these four steps every time:

  1. Solve until the variable is alone on the left (for example, x > 6).
  2. Find the boundary number on the line and put your dot there.
  3. Choose the dot. Use an open dot ○ for < or > (the boundary is not a solution). Use a closed dot ● for ≤ or ≥ (the boundary is a solution).
  4. Shade with an arrow toward every number that works — to the right for > or ≥, to the left for < or ≤.

💡 Quick dot check: does the symbol have a little line under it (≤ or ≥)? Line under → closed dot ●. Plain < or > → open dot ○.

✨ Worked Examples

“A tank holds at most 50 gal. It has 18. How many more (g)?”
  1. 18 + g ≤ 50
  2. Subtract 18 → g ≤ 32 gallons
  3. “At most” → closed dot ● at 32, shade left
Graph:
“Pollutants must stay below 30 ppm. At 12 ppm, each step adds 2. Steps (p)?”
  1. 12 + 2p < 30
  2. Subtract 12 → 2p < 18
  3. Divide by 2 → p < 9 steps
  4. “Below” → open dot ○ at 9, shade left
Graph:
“Terria needs at least 90 pts. She has 65; each quiz is 5. Quizzes (q)?”
  1. 65 + 5q ≥ 90
  2. Subtract 65 → 5q ≥ 25
  3. Divide by 5 → q ≥ 5 quizzes
  4. “At least” → closed dot ● at 5, shade right
Graph:
💡 Read the phrase carefully — “at most” and “at least” point the sign in opposite directions.
